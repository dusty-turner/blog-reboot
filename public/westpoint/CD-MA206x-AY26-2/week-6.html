Lesson-by-Lesson Focus

Lesson 21 — One-Sample t-Test

TipTakeaway
  • The five-step hypothesis testing framework: hypotheses → conditions → test statistic → p-value → conclusion
  • Test statistic: \(t = \frac{\bar{x} - \mu_0}{s/\sqrt{n}}\)
  • Use the \(t\)-distribution with \(n-1\) degrees of freedom
  • The process is the same every time — the details change, the framework doesn’t
ImportantAssessment Focus
  • Complete write-up: Every test answer must include all five steps: (1) hypotheses with correct notation, (2) conditions checked, (3) test statistic computed, (4) p-value stated, (5) conclusion in context. Missing any step should cost points.
  • Conclusion language: Must reference the alternative hypothesis and the context. “We have sufficient evidence to conclude that…” — not just “reject \(H_0\).”
  • Conditions: Random sample, independence, \(n \geq 30\) or population approximately normal. Must be stated explicitly.

Lesson 22 — One-Proportion Z-Test

TipTakeaway
  • Hypotheses involve the population proportion \(p\), not \(\hat{p}\)
  • Test statistic: \(z = \frac{\hat{p} - p_0}{\sqrt{p_0(1-p_0)/n}}\)
  • Conditions: random sample, independence, \(np_0 \geq 10\) and \(n(1-p_0) \geq 10\)
ImportantAssessment Focus
  • SE under \(H_0\): Under \(H_0\), use \(p_0\) (not \(\hat{p}\)) in the SE formula. This is a very common error on WebAssign and WPRs.
  • Success/failure condition: Must check \(np_0 \geq 10\) and \(n(1-p_0) \geq 10\) using the null value, not the sample proportion.
  • Practical vs. statistical significance: A statistically significant result can be practically meaningless with large \(n\). Push cadets to interpret the magnitude, not just the p-value.

Lesson 23 — Two-Sample t-Test

TipTakeaway
  • Comparing means from two independent groups
  • Test statistic: \(t = \frac{(\bar{x}_1 - \bar{x}_2) - 0}{\sqrt{s_1^2/n_1 + s_2^2/n_2}}\)
  • Conditions must be checked for both groups independently
  • Choosing the right test: one sample or two? Means or proportions?
ImportantAssessment Focus
  • Test selection: Given a word problem, can the cadet pick the right test? This is the single most important WPR 2 skill.
  • Conditions for both groups: Independence, random sampling, and sample size conditions must be verified for each group separately.
  • Interpretation: “There is a significant difference in mean [response] between [group 1] and [group 2]” — must be contextual.

Lesson 24 — Paired t-Test

TipTakeaway
  • Used when the same subjects are measured twice (before/after, matched pairs)
  • Take differences first, then do a one-sample t-test on the differences
  • Test statistic: \(t = \frac{\bar{d} - 0}{s_d/\sqrt{n}}\)
  • Identifying paired designs is the key skill
ImportantAssessment Focus
  • Paired vs. independent: If the problem says “before and after” or “the same subjects,” it’s paired. Watch for cadets who compute two separate means and do a two-sample test instead of taking differences first.
  • Direction of differences: Cadets must define the direction (e.g., Before − After) and keep it consistent with the hypotheses.
  • Lost power: Using a two-sample test on paired data loses power — cadets should understand why pairing matters.

TEE-Style Question

CautionWhen All Is Said and Done…

Cadets should be able to answer a question like this:

A commander wants to know if a new PT program improves 2-mile run times. She records the run times (in minutes) of 12 soldiers before and after the 8-week program.

Soldier Before After
Mean 16.8 15.9
SD 2.1 1.9
\(\bar{d}\) (Before − After) 0.9
\(s_d\) 1.4

  1. Why is a paired t-test appropriate here instead of a two-sample t-test?

  2. Conduct a hypothesis test at \(\alpha = 0.05\) to determine if the program significantly reduces run times. Include all five steps.

  3. A separate study finds that 38 out of 200 infantry soldiers and 22 out of 150 artillery soldiers failed their last PT test. Test whether the failure proportions differ at \(\alpha = 0.05\).

Teaching Demo

NoteSuggested Classroom Demonstration

The Decision Flowchart (use all week)

Build this on the board and add to it each lesson:

Start → How many samples?
  → 1 sample → Means or proportions?
       → Means: One-sample t-test
       → Proportions: One-proportion z-test
  → 2 samples → Same subjects or different?
       → Same (paired): Paired t-test
       → Different (independent): Two-sample t-test

Complete Hypothesis Test Walkthrough (Lesson 21): Use a military fitness example: “A commander claims the average run time for her company is under 14 minutes. A sample of 25 soldiers has \(\bar{x} = 13.6\) min and \(s = 1.8\) min.”

Walk through all five steps on the board. Have cadets identify what would change if this were a proportion (“What fraction run under 14 min?”) or paired (“Same soldiers tested before and after a training program”).
