Lesson-by-Lesson Focus

Lesson 17 — Central Limit Theorem

TipTakeaway
  • Central Limit Theorem (CLT): If \(X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_n\) are iid with mean \(\mu\) and standard deviation \(\sigma\), then for large \(n\): \(\bar{X} \sim N\left(\mu, \frac{\sigma^2}{n}\right)\)
  • Standard Error of the Mean: \(\sigma_{\bar{X}} = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\)
  • Works regardless of population shape (for large enough \(n\))
  • \(n \geq 30\) is the common rule of thumb (any \(n\) if population is normal)
  • Larger \(n\) → smaller SE → more precise estimates
ImportantAssessment Focus
  • CLT application: Can cadets compute \(P(\bar{X} > c)\) by standardizing with \(\sigma/\sqrt{n}\), not \(\sigma\)? Using \(\sigma\) instead of the standard error is the most common CLT mistake.
  • When CLT applies: Cadets must state the sample size condition — \(n \geq 30\) or population is approximately normal.
  • This is the bridge from probability to inference — spend time on the intuition, not just the formula.

Lesson 18 — Confidence Intervals I

TipTakeaway
  • Large sample (\(n \geq 30\)): \(\bar{X} \pm z_{\alpha/2} \cdot \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\)
  • Small sample (\(n < 30\)): \(\bar{X} \pm t_{\alpha/2, n-1} \cdot \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}\)
  • Margin of error: \(E = z_{\alpha/2} \cdot \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}\) (or \(t_{\alpha/2, n-1} \cdot \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}\))
  • Higher confidence level → wider interval
  • Larger sample size → narrower interval
ImportantAssessment Focus
  • CI interpretation wording: This is non-negotiable. The correct form is: “We are 95% confident that the true [parameter] is between [lower] and [upper].” Deduct for: “There is a 95% chance that \(\mu\) is in this interval” or “95% of the data falls in this interval.”
  • z vs t justification: Cadets must state why they chose z or t. If \(\sigma\) is unknown (almost always), it must be t. Watch for cadets defaulting to z because it’s “easier.”
  • Conditions check: Every CI requires: (1) random sample, (2) independence, (3) sample size condition (CLT). These must appear in the solution.

Lesson 19 — Confidence Intervals II

TipTakeaway
  • Interpret confidence intervals correctly in context
  • Construct confidence intervals for proportions
  • Assess robustness of CI assumptions
  • The \(t\)-Distribution: Has heavier tails to account for extra uncertainty when \(s\) is an unreliable estimate of \(\sigma\)
ImportantAssessment Focus
  • Proportion CI conditions: \(n\hat{p} \geq 10\) and \(n(1-\hat{p}) \geq 10\). Cadets must check these before constructing a proportion CI.
  • Interpretation precision: “We are 95% confident the true proportion of cadets who…” — not “95% of cadets…”
  • Margin of error interpretation: Cadets should connect margin of error to the width of the interval and sample size.

Lesson 20 — Intro to Hypothesis Testing

TipTakeaway
  • Hypothesis testing framework: \(H_0\), \(H_a\), test statistic, p-value, decision
  • \(H_0\) is the status quo; \(H_a\) is what we’re trying to find evidence for
  • p-value: probability of observing a result this extreme or more extreme, assuming \(H_0\) is true
  • Decision: reject \(H_0\) if p-value \(< \alpha\); fail to reject otherwise
ImportantAssessment Focus
  • Hypothesis notation: \(H_0\) and \(H_a\) must be written in terms of the population parameter (\(\mu\), \(p\)), not the sample statistic (\(\bar{x}\), \(\hat{p}\)).
  • p-value interpretation: “The probability of observing a result this extreme or more extreme, assuming \(H_0\) is true.” NOT “the probability that \(H_0\) is true.”
  • Conclusion language: “Fail to reject \(H_0\)” — never “accept \(H_0\).” This distinction is critical.

TEE-Style Question

CautionWhen All Is Said and Done…

Cadets should be able to answer a question like this:

A random sample of 36 cadets has a mean study time of \(\bar{x} = 14.2\) hours per week with \(s = 4.8\) hours.

  1. Construct a 95% confidence interval for the true mean study time. State the conditions required and verify them.

  2. Interpret the confidence interval in context.

  3. A department head claims cadets study at least 16 hours per week. Conduct a hypothesis test at \(\alpha = 0.05\). State hypotheses, compute the test statistic and p-value, and write a conclusion in context.

  4. Is your conclusion in (c) consistent with the CI in (a)? Explain.

Teaching Demo

NoteSuggested Classroom Demonstration

CLT Simulation (Lesson 17)

In R (or live on the board), demonstrate the CLT:

  1. Start with a clearly non-Normal population (e.g., Exponential with \(\lambda = 1\))
  2. Take 1,000 samples of size \(n = 5\), compute \(\bar{x}\) for each — plot the distribution
  3. Repeat with \(n = 30\) — show it’s approximately Normal
  4. Connect: “This is why we can do inference about \(\mu\) even when the population isn’t Normal”

Building a CI from Scratch (Lesson 18): Using class data (e.g., hours of sleep), compute \(\bar{x}\) and \(s\), find \(t^*\) from the table, and construct the interval by hand: \[\bar{x} \pm t^* \cdot \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}\]

Then interpret it correctly as a class. Have a cadet give the interpretation and let the class critique it.
