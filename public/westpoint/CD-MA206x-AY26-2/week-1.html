Lesson-by-Lesson Focus

Lesson 1 — Types of Data, Sampling, & Study Design

TipTakeaway
  • Population vs Sample vs Process
  • Parameters vs Statistics
  • Categorical vs Quantitative variables
ImportantAssessment Focus
  • Notation: Are cadets distinguishing parameters (\(\mu\), \(\sigma\), \(p\)) from statistics (\(\bar{x}\), \(s\), \(\hat{p}\))? This is the foundation — correct it now.
  • Variable classification: Cadets must correctly classify variables as categorical vs quantitative, and discrete vs continuous. Watch for confusing “numerical categories” (jersey numbers) with quantitative variables.
  • Sampling methods: Can they identify SRS, stratified, cluster, systematic, and convenience sampling in context?

Lesson 2 — Sampling & Study Design

TipTakeaway
  • Stem-and-leaf displays: preserve data while showing shape
  • Dotplots: simple visual for small datasets
  • Histograms: frequency distributions for larger datasets
  • Describing distributions: Shape, Center, Spread, Outliers (S-C-S-O)
ImportantAssessment Focus
  • S-C-S-O: Every distribution description must address all four: Shape, Center, Spread, Outliers. Deduct for incomplete descriptions.
  • Study design: Can cadets distinguish observational studies from experiments? Do they understand random assignment enables causal conclusions?
  • Graph interpretation: Answers should go beyond “the histogram shows the data” — they must connect the visualization to a story.

Lesson 3 — Measures of Location

TipTakeaway
  • Mean (\(\bar{x}\)): Arithmetic average; uses all data but sensitive to outliers
  • Median (\(\tilde{x}\)): Middle value; resistant to outliers
  • Mode: Most frequent value; useful for categorical data
  • Percentiles and Quartiles: Q1, Q2 (median), Q3 divide data into four parts
  • Five-number summary: Min, Q1, Median, Q3, Max
ImportantAssessment Focus
  • Mean vs median choice: Cadets must justify which measure of center is more appropriate given the data’s shape and outliers.
  • Contextual interpretation: “The median commute time is 28 minutes” not just “\(\tilde{x} = 28\).”
  • Percentile interpretation: “The 75th percentile means 75% of observations fall at or below this value.”

Lesson 4 — Measures of Variability

TipTakeaway
  • Range: Max − Min; simple but sensitive to outliers
  • IQR: Q3 − Q1; resistant to outliers, measures middle 50%
  • 1.5 × IQR Rule: Values beyond \(Q_1 - 1.5 \times \text{IQR}\) or \(Q_3 + 1.5 \times \text{IQR}\) are potential outliers
  • Variance (\(s^2\)): Average squared deviation from the mean
  • Standard Deviation (\(s\)): Square root of variance; in original units
ImportantAssessment Focus
  • SD interpretation in context: “The standard deviation is 3.2 inches” not just “SD = 3.2.” Must include units and meaning.
  • Variance/SD calculations: Watch for skipped steps — forgetting to square deviations, dividing by \(n\) instead of \(n-1\).
  • Pairing measures: Report IQR with median (for skewed data); report SD with mean (for symmetric data). Deduct for mismatched pairings.
  • WebAssign: Check that cadets are showing reasonable completion rates in the first week. Flag anyone with zero submissions early.

TEE-Style Question

CautionWhen All Is Said and Done…

Cadets should be able to answer a question like this:

A researcher collects data on the commute times (in minutes) of 8 randomly selected employees: 22, 35, 28, 41, 19, 33, 27, 30.

  1. Classify the variable “commute time” as categorical or quantitative, and as discrete or continuous.

  2. Compute the sample mean \(\bar{x}\) and sample standard deviation \(s\).

  3. Interpret the standard deviation in the context of this problem.

  4. Would the mean or median be a more appropriate measure of center for this dataset? Justify your answer.

Teaching Demo

NoteSuggested Classroom Demonstration

Population vs. Sample — Class Data Activity (Lesson 1)

Collect a quick variable from the class (e.g., hours of sleep last night). Compute \(\bar{x}\) and \(s\) on the board. Then ask: “Is this a parameter or a statistic? What is the population we care about?”

Use this to reinforce:

  • The class is a sample, not the population
  • \(\bar{x}\) estimates \(\mu\), \(s\) estimates \(\sigma\)
  • Different samples give different values — this motivates the entire course

Board Problem to Walk Through (Lessons 3–4): Given a small dataset (5–7 values), compute the mean, median, variance, and SD step by step. Emphasize the \(n-1\) denominator and ask cadets why we divide by \(n-1\) (degrees of freedom preview).
