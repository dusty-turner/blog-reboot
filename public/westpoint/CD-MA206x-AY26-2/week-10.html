Lesson-by-Lesson Focus

Lesson 36 — ANOVA I: One-Way

TipTakeaway
  • ANOVA is an extension of the two-sample t-test to 3+ groups
  • The F-statistic compares between-group variability to within-group variability: \(F = \frac{MSB}{MSW}\)
  • ANOVA table: SS, df, MS, F, p-value
  • A significant F-test means at least one group mean differs — but doesn’t say which
  • Why not multiple t-tests? Inflated Type I error rate
ImportantAssessment Focus
  • ANOVA table completion: Cadets should be able to fill in missing values given partial information (SS, df → MS → F).
  • Hypotheses: \(H_0: \mu_1 = \mu_2 = \cdots = \mu_k\) vs \(H_a\): at least one mean differs.
  • Post-hoc requirement: A significant F-test requires follow-up (e.g., Tukey’s HSD). Cadets must know they can’t stop at “reject \(H_0\).”
  • Assumptions: Independence, normality within groups, equal variances across groups. Same as t-tests.

Lesson 38 — ANOVA II: Two-Way

TipTakeaway
  • Two-way ANOVA: two factors examined simultaneously
  • Main effects + interaction effect (same concept as MLR interactions)
  • Interaction: the effect of one factor depends on the level of the other
  • Connect back to MLR interactions from Lesson 33
ImportantAssessment Focus
  • Interaction interpretation: Can cadets explain what it means for two factors to interact in an ANOVA context?
  • Reading two-way ANOVA output: Multiple F-tests — one for each main effect and one for the interaction. Cadets should know to check interaction first.
  • Connection to regression: Two-way ANOVA is equivalent to MLR with indicators and interactions. Help cadets see the connection.

TEE-Style Question

CautionWhen All Is Said and Done…

Cadets should be able to answer a question like this:

Three different rifle cleaning methods are tested. 18 soldiers are randomly assigned (6 per method) and their cleaning times (in minutes) are recorded. An ANOVA table is partially shown:

Source SS df MS F p-value
Between 84.3 ? ? ? ?
Within 112.5 ? ?
Total 196.8 ?

  1. Complete the ANOVA table (fill in all missing values).

  2. At \(\alpha = 0.05\), is there a significant difference in mean cleaning time among the three methods? State hypotheses and conclusion.

  3. What are the assumptions for this test? How would you check them?

  4. If the F-test is significant, what is the next step? Why can’t we just use multiple t-tests?

Teaching Demo

NoteSuggested Classroom Demonstration

One-Way ANOVA Walkthrough (Lesson 36)

Use a military example: “Three training programs are compared. 15 soldiers are randomly assigned (5 per program). Their fitness improvement scores are recorded.”

On the board or in R:

  1. Show side-by-side boxplots of the three groups
  2. Compute group means and the overall mean
  3. Walk through the ANOVA table: SSB, SSW, SST, df, MS, F
  4. Compute the F-statistic and find the p-value
  5. Interpret: “At least one training program produces a different mean improvement”
  6. Discuss: “But which one? We need Tukey’s HSD or similar post-hoc test”

Presentation Feedback (Lesson 37 IPR): Model what good feedback looks like. Show a sample slide and have instructors practice giving feedback on: (1) statistical accuracy, (2) clarity of communication, (3) visual design. This calibrates feedback quality before cadets present.
