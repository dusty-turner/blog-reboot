Lesson-by-Lesson Focus

Lesson 28 — Simple Linear Regression I

TipTakeaway
  • Least squares regression: \(\hat{y} = b_0 + b_1 x\)
  • Slope interpretation: “For each 1-unit increase in \(x\), the predicted \(y\) changes by \(b_1\)”
  • Intercept interpretation: “When \(x = 0\), the predicted \(y\) is \(b_0\)” (if \(x = 0\) is meaningful)
  • R command: lm(y ~ x, data = df)
ImportantAssessment Focus
  • Slope interpretation in context: The slope must be interpreted with units and context every time. “For each additional year of experience, salary increases by approximately $2,400 on average” — not “the slope is 2400.”
  • Intercept caveat: If \(x = 0\) is outside the data range, the intercept may not have a meaningful interpretation. Cadets should note this.

Lesson 30 — Simple Linear Regression II

TipTakeaway
  • \(R^2\): proportion of variability in \(y\) explained by \(x\)
  • Residual plots: the most important diagnostic tool
  • Residuals = observed − predicted: \(e_i = y_i - \hat{y}_i\)
  • Look for patterns in residual plots: curvature, fanning, clusters
ImportantAssessment Focus
  • \(R^2\) interpretation: Must say “X% of the variability in [response] is explained by [predictor].” NOT “the model is X% accurate.”
  • Residual plot reading: Cadets should identify patterns (curvature, fanning, clusters) and state what they imply (non-linearity, non-constant variance, subgroups).
  • Prediction vs. extrapolation: If a cadet predicts \(\hat{y}\) for an \(x\) outside the observed range, they should flag it as extrapolation and note the risk.

Lesson 31 — Multiple Linear Regression I

TipTakeaway
  • MLR extends SLR to multiple predictors: \(\hat{y} = b_0 + b_1 x_1 + b_2 x_2 + \cdots\)
  • Each coefficient is interpreted “holding all other variables constant”
  • Regression shows association, not causation, without proper experimental design
  • Confounding: a lurking variable that is associated with both the predictor and the response
ImportantAssessment Focus
  • “Holding all else constant”: Every MLR coefficient interpretation must include this qualifier. Deduct if missing.
  • Causality language: Watch for cadets saying “\(x\) causes \(y\)” from observational regression. Regression ≠ causation.
  • Tech Report draft quality: With the report due at Lesson 36, check that cadets have a regression analysis in their project — not just descriptive stats.

TEE-Style Question

CautionWhen All Is Said and Done…

Cadets should be able to answer a question like this:

A regression of fuel efficiency (MPG) on vehicle weight (1000s of lbs) yields:

\[\widehat{\text{MPG}} = 45.2 - 5.8 \cdot \text{Weight}\]

with \(R^2 = 0.74\), \(n = 30\), and \(s_e = 3.1\).

  1. Interpret the slope in context.

  2. Interpret \(R^2\) in context.

  3. A car weighs 4,500 lbs. Predict its MPG. Is this interpolation or extrapolation? (Data range: 2,000–5,000 lbs.)

  4. The residual plot shows a clear U-shaped pattern. What does this indicate, and what should you do?

  5. A second model adds Cylinders as a predictor, giving \(R^2 = 0.81\). Does adding this variable improve the model? What additional information would you need to make a stronger argument?

Teaching Demo

NoteSuggested Classroom Demonstration

From Scatterplot to Regression Equation (Lesson 28)

Use a real dataset (e.g., car weight vs. MPG, or cadet study hours vs. exam score). On the board:

  1. Plot the scatterplot (or show in R)
  2. Eyeball a “best fit” line — have cadets estimate the slope and intercept
  3. Show the least squares solution in R: lm(y ~ x, data = df)
  4. Interpret the slope and intercept in context
  5. Compute \(R^2\) and interpret it
  6. Plot residuals vs. fitted values — ask “what are we looking for?”

Causality Discussion (Lesson 31): Present this scenario: “A regression of ice cream sales on drowning deaths shows a strong positive relationship (\(R^2 = 0.85\)). Does ice cream cause drowning?”

Use this to introduce confounding (temperature) and motivate why MLR matters — we want to “control for” other variables. Connect to DAGs if the class is ready.
