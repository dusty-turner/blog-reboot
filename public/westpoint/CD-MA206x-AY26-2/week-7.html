Lesson-by-Lesson Focus

Lesson 25 — Two Population Proportions

TipTakeaway
  • Comparing proportions from two independent groups
  • Test statistic uses the pooled proportion \(\hat{p} = \frac{x_1 + x_2}{n_1 + n_2}\) under \(H_0\)
  • CI uses unpooled SE with individual \(\hat{p}_1\) and \(\hat{p}_2\)
  • Conditions: random samples, independence, success/failure condition for both groups
ImportantAssessment Focus
  • Pooled vs unpooled SE: For hypothesis tests, SE uses pooled \(\hat{p}\). For CIs, it uses the individual \(\hat{p}_1\) and \(\hat{p}_2\). This is a common mix-up — grade it strictly.
  • Conditions for both groups: Success/failure condition (\(n\hat{p} \geq 10\), \(n(1-\hat{p}) \geq 10\)) must be checked for each group.
  • Hypotheses format: Must use population parameters (\(p_1 - p_2\)), not sample statistics.

Lesson 26 — Review (Lessons 17–25)

TipTakeaway
  • Test selection is the #1 skill: given a scenario, which test applies?
  • Decision flowchart: How many samples? → Means or proportions? → Paired or independent?
  • Summary table of all tests: parameter, hypotheses, test statistic, conditions
ImportantAssessment Focus
  • Test identification: Focus review on scenario-based practice where cadets must name the test and justify why.
  • Common confusion points: paired vs independent, pooled vs unpooled SE, z vs t.
  • This is the single best WPR prep activity — give 6–8 scenarios and have cadets classify.

Lesson 27 — WPR 2 (175 pts)

ImportantWPR 2 Grading Focus
  • Test identification: Every problem should begin with the cadet naming the test and justifying why. If they pick the wrong test, cap partial credit regardless of subsequent work.
  • Hypotheses format: Must use population parameters (\(\mu_1 - \mu_2\), \(p_1 - p_2\)), not sample statistics.
  • Conclusion in context: Full sentence referencing the research question. “Reject” or “fail to reject” language required.
  • Conditions: For two-sample and two-proportion tests, conditions must be checked for both groups.
  • Rubric calibration: Grade by problem, not by cadet. Align on partial credit before grading begins.

TEE-Style Question

CautionWhen All Is Said and Done…

Cadets should be able to answer a question like this:

In a study of two recruiting methods, 84 out of 300 applicants contacted by Method A accepted, while 62 out of 250 contacted by Method B accepted.

  1. State the hypotheses to test whether the acceptance proportions differ between the two methods.

  2. Compute the pooled proportion \(\hat{p}\) and the test statistic.

  3. Find the p-value and state your conclusion at \(\alpha = 0.05\).

  4. Construct a 95% confidence interval for \(p_1 - p_2\). Note: which SE formula should you use here, and why is it different from part (b)?

Teaching Demo

NoteSuggested Classroom Demonstration

Test Selection Practice (Lesson 26 Review)

Give cadets 6–8 short scenarios (2–3 sentences each) and have them identify:

  1. The parameter of interest (\(\mu\), \(p\), \(\mu_1 - \mu_2\), \(p_1 - p_2\), \(\mu_d\))
  2. The appropriate test
  3. The hypotheses

Do this as a think-pair-share. Scenarios should include tricky cases:

  • “A new drug is tested on 50 patients; 32 improve. Is this better than the historical 50% rate?” → one-proportion z
  • “20 cadets’ PT scores are recorded before and after a 6-week program” → paired t
  • “Two platoons are compared on marksmanship scores” → two-sample t
  • “Is the proportion of left-handed soldiers different between infantry and artillery?” → two-proportion z

This is the single best WPR prep activity.
