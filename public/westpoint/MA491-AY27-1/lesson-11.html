Lessons 11–14: Semi-Independent Project

Project Overview

You just finished the NYC 311 guided project. You learned how to acquire data, clean it, explore it, model it, and build a dashboard. Now you do it again — but this time you pick the topic and drive the bus.

The arc is the same four lessons: get and clean, explore and model, iterate and refine, build and deploy. The difference is autonomy. There is no starter code. There is no pre-cleaned dataset. You choose a question, find data that can answer it, and build something worth showing.

Use AI aggressively to help you work through problems — but own every line of code you produce. If you cannot explain it, rewrite it until you can.

Suggested Topics

Pick one of these, or propose your own (clear it with the instructor by the end of Lesson 11):

  • Sports analytics — NFL play-by-play, NCAA basketball, Army football stats
  • Public health data — CDC WONDER, WHO Global Health Observatory, COVID tracking
  • Military installations / force structure — DMDC data, base locations, unit structure
  • Weather & climate data — NOAA, OpenWeatherMap API, historical extremes
  • College Scorecard / education data — graduation rates, earnings, institutional comparisons
  • Reddit / social media analysis — subreddit trends, sentiment analysis, post engagement
  • Any public API or dataset that interests you — convince me it has enough depth for four lessons

The best projects start with a question you actually care about.

Lesson 11: Get & Clean the Data

Goal: Walk out of class with a clean, analysis-ready dataset and a clear project question.

75-Minute Outline

Time Activity
0–10 min Topic selection. Finalize your topic. If you already know, great. If not, browse the suggested list and talk it through with a classmate or the instructor. Lock it in.
10–25 min Find and acquire data. Locate your data source. Download it, hit the API, or scrape it (ethically). Document where it came from and what each variable means.
25–50 min Clean and wrangle. Get the data into a tidy format. Handle missing values, fix data types, rename columns, filter to the subset you need. This is where most of the work lives.
50–65 min Initial look. Produce 2–3 summary statistics or quick plots that confirm the data is what you think it is. Identify anything weird early.
65–75 min Document. Write a brief paragraph in your Quarto doc: what is the data, where did it come from, what question are you trying to answer?
TipAI Assist

Use AI to help you figure out how to read unfamiliar file formats, parse API responses, or debug cleaning code. Paste your glimpse() output and ask it to suggest cleaning steps.

Lesson 12: Explore & Model

Goal: Understand the structure of your data through EDA and fit an initial model.

75-Minute Outline

Time Activity
0–10 min Review. Revisit your clean data. Make sure Lesson 11 work is solid. Fix anything that broke overnight.
10–30 min Exploratory data analysis. Build visualizations tailored to your data. Distributions, relationships, trends over time — whatever makes sense for your question.
30–50 min Fit a model. Pick an appropriate model for your question. Linear regression, logistic regression, classification tree, clustering — match the method to the problem. Run it. Look at diagnostics.
50–65 min Interpret results. What did the model find? What surprised you? What does not make sense yet? Write it down.
65–75 min Plan next steps. Identify 2–3 things to improve: a better feature, a different model, a visualization that tells the story more clearly.
TipAI Assist

If you are unsure which model fits your problem, describe your data and question to AI and ask it to suggest approaches. Then read about those approaches before blindly implementing them.

Lesson 13: Iterate & Refine

Goal: Improve your analysis based on what you learned in Lesson 12. Make it presentation-ready.

75-Minute Outline

Time Activity
0–10 min Assess. Look at your Lesson 12 output with fresh eyes. What is the weakest part? Start there.
10–35 min Improve the model or analysis. Try the better feature. Fit the alternative model. Engineer a new variable. Compare results to your first attempt.
35–55 min Refine visualizations. Make your plots clear, labeled, and honest. Every plot should answer a specific question. Remove chart junk. Add context.
55–70 min Build the narrative. Your Quarto document should now read as a coherent story: question, data, exploration, model, findings. Fill in the gaps.
70–75 min Prep for dashboard. Decide which 3–4 elements will go on your dashboard. Sketch the layout.
TipAI Assist

Ask AI to critique your visualizations. Paste your ggplot code and ask for specific improvements — better color scales, clearer labels, more informative titles.

Lesson 14: Build & Deploy Dashboard

Goal: Build an interactive dashboard and present your work to the class.

75-Minute Outline

Time Activity
0–5 min Setup. Open your project. Review your dashboard plan from Lesson 13.
5–35 min Build the dashboard. Create your Quarto dashboard with 3–4 key panels. Include at least one interactive element (plotly, DT table, or Shiny widget).
35–45 min Deploy. Push to GitHub and deploy via Netlify or Quarto Pub. Confirm the live link works.
45–75 min Presentations. Each person gives a 3–4 minute walkthrough of their dashboard. Cover: what question you asked, what the data showed, and one thing that surprised you. Class asks questions.
TipAI Assist

If your dashboard layout is fighting you, paste your YAML and error messages into AI. Dashboard formatting issues are exactly the kind of problem AI solves quickly.

Deliverables

By the end of Lesson 14, you will submit:

  1. Quarto project on GitHub — all code, data (or data-fetching script), and rendered output
  2. Deployed dashboard — live link that the instructor can visit and interact with
  3. Brief write-up (embedded in your Quarto doc) covering:
    • Your question and why you chose it
    • Where the data came from
    • Key findings (with supporting visuals)
    • One limitation or thing you would do differently with more time
ImportantExpectations

You have more autonomy on this project than on NYC 311. That means more freedom and more responsibility. The standard is the same: you must be able to explain every line of your code and every choice in your analysis. Use AI to accelerate your work, not to replace your thinking.
