Lessons 25–30: Passion Project & Demo Day

Project Overview

This is YOUR project. You choose the topic, the approach, the deliverable. Everything you have learned this semester — data wrangling, visualization, modeling, web scraping, dashboards, Git, hosting — converges here.

The requirements are simple:

  • It must be technically substantive — not a toy example, but something that solves a real problem or answers a real question.
  • It must be hosted — a live URL that anyone can visit.
  • It must be presentable to a non-technical audience — your grandmother, your company commander, a visiting general. If they can’t understand what it does and why it matters, it’s not done.

Lesson 30 is Demo Day. You will present your project to invited guests — faculty, officers, peers, and possibly external visitors. This is the capstone of the course and the centerpiece of your portfolio.

You have earned this. Everything before this was training. This is the real thing.

Lesson 25: Proposal & Scoping

NoteObjective

Leave this lesson with an approved project proposal and a realistic plan for the next four build lessons.

What You Do Today

  1. Write a 1-page project proposal that answers:

    • What is the question or problem?
    • What is the data source? (Do you have it? Can you get it?)
    • What is the plan? (What tools, what approach?)
    • What is the deliverable? (Shiny app? Dashboard? Automated pipeline? Game?)

  2. Get instructor feedback and approval. We will talk through every proposal today. The goal is to make sure your scope is realistic — ambitious enough to be impressive, tight enough to finish in four lessons.

  3. Scope it realistically. You have four 75-minute build sessions. That is roughly 5 hours of in-class work time. Plan accordingly.

Examples of Good Scope

  • A Shiny app that lets users explore a dataset interactively
  • A dashboard that tracks and visualizes something you care about
  • A tool that automates something tedious (data cleaning, report generation, web scraping)
  • A prediction model deployed as an interactive web app
  • A game built with Shiny or another framework
  • A data pipeline that ingests, transforms, and presents information
  • An analysis that tells a compelling story through a polished, hosted document

Examples of Bad Scope

  • “I want to build a social media platform” — too big
  • “I want to make a bar chart” — too small
  • “I’ll figure it out as I go” — not a plan
TipAI Policy

AI is still encouraged throughout this block. The standard is the same as it has been all semester: own every line, defend every choice. If you can’t explain it, you can’t ship it.

Lesson 28: Iterate & Refine

NoteObjective

Move from “it works” to “it works well.” Start thinking about your presentation narrative.

What You Do Today

  • Core functionality should be complete
  • Now focus on what separates good from great:
    • Handle edge cases
    • Improve UX — labels, layout, responsiveness
    • Polish visualizations — colors, annotations, clarity
    • Strengthen the storytelling — what is the narrative arc of your project?
  • Start thinking about Demo Day. What will you say? What will you show? What’s the one thing you want your audience to remember?

Before you leave: commit to Git.

75-Minute Outline

Time Activity
0–5 Instructor framing: the difference between “done” and “great”
5–55 Iterate and refine (instructor available)
55–70 Pair up: describe your project to a partner in 2 minutes. Get feedback.
70–75 Git commit

Lesson 29: Final Polish & Hosting

NoteObjective

Ship it. Your project should be live, on your portfolio, and ready to present.

What You Do Today

  • Final touches on the project — last bug fixes, last visual tweaks
  • Deploy and host the final version at a live URL
  • Add to your portfolio website — this is now a permanent part of your professional presence
  • Rehearse your presentation — practice with a partner, get feedback, tighten the narrative
  • Commit the final version to Git

By the end of today, everything should be done. Tomorrow is Demo Day — you should walk in ready to present, not ready to debug.

Lesson 30: Demo Day — Presentations to Invited Guests

ImportantDemo Day

This is it. Show what you built.

Format

  • 10-minute presentations per cadet: 5 minutes of live demo + 5 minutes of Q&A
  • Live demo of your hosted project — not slides, not screenshots. The real thing.
  • Schedule depends on class size; exact order will be posted the day before.

Audience

Invited faculty, officers, peers, and possibly external guests. These are people who want to see what you can do. Show them.

Expectations

  • Dress: Professional
  • Delivery: Clear, confident, concise. You built this — own it.
  • Demo: Live. Working. If something breaks, handle it gracefully.
  • Q&A: Be ready to explain your technical choices, your data, your approach. You should be able to defend every decision.

What a Great Presentation Looks Like

  1. The hook — What problem did you solve? Why does it matter? (30 seconds)
  2. The demo — Walk through the project live. Show the key features. Let it speak for itself. (3–4 minutes)
  3. The reflection — What did you learn? What would you do differently? What are you proud of? (30 seconds–1 minute)
  4. Q&A — Engage with questions. Be honest about limitations.
