Lesson Deep Dive

Lesson 17 — Central Limit Theorem

Objectives (SLOs 3, 5):

  • State CLT conditions for \(\bar{X}\)
  • Use normal approximations to sampling distributions
  • Relate accuracy to \(n\) and population shape
TipKey Concepts
  • Central Limit Theorem: If \(X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_n\) are iid with mean \(\mu\) and SD \(\sigma\), then for large \(n\): \(\bar{X} \dot{\sim} N\left(\mu, \frac{\sigma^2}{n}\right)\)
  • Standard Error of the Mean: \(\sigma_{\bar{X}} = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\)
  • Works regardless of population shape (for large enough \(n\))
  • \(n \geq 30\) is the common rule of thumb (any \(n\) if population is normal)
  • Larger \(n\) → smaller SE → more precise estimates
ImportantGrading Focus
  • SE vs SD: The single most common CLT mistake is using \(\sigma\) instead of \(\sigma/\sqrt{n}\). If cadets standardize with \(\sigma\) alone, they are computing \(P(X > c)\), not \(P(\bar{X} > c)\).
  • CLT conditions: Cadets must state the sample size condition — \(n \geq 30\) or population is approximately normal.
  • Distribution of \(\bar{X}\): Cadets should write “\(\bar{X} \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2/n)\)” explicitly before computing probabilities.
NoteDeeper Material

Why the CLT works (intuition): Consider the sum \(S_n = X_1 + \cdots + X_n\). Each \(X_i\) contributes a “bump” of randomness. When you add many independent bumps, the positive and negative deviations average out, and what’s left converges to a bell curve. The formal proof uses moment-generating functions or characteristic functions, but the intuition is about cancellation of independent fluctuations.

CLT for proportions: If \(X \sim \text{Binomial}(n, p)\), then \(\hat{p} = X/n\) and the CLT gives \(\hat{p} \dot{\sim} N\left(p, \frac{p(1-p)}{n}\right)\). This is the foundation for all proportion inference in Lessons 19, 22, and 25. Making this connection explicit now saves time later.

Berry-Esseen Theorem: The CLT convergence rate is \(O(1/\sqrt{n})\). For highly skewed populations, \(n = 30\) may not be enough — the rule of thumb is conservative for symmetric populations and optimistic for skewed ones. In practice, look at the data.

Sampling distribution vs population distribution: This distinction is conceptually the hardest part of the course. The population distribution describes individual observations; the sampling distribution describes what happens to a statistic across repeated samples. Spend time on this — it’s the key to understanding p-values.

Lesson 18 — Confidence Intervals I

Objectives (SLOs 5, 6):

  • Define confidence level and margin of error
  • Construct one-sample confidence intervals for means
  • Justify z vs. t procedures and state conditions
TipKey Concepts
  • Large sample (\(n \geq 30\)): \(\bar{x} \pm z_{\alpha/2} \cdot \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}\)
  • Small sample (\(n < 30\)): \(\bar{x} \pm t_{\alpha/2, n-1} \cdot \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}\)
  • Margin of error: \(E = z_{\alpha/2} \cdot \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}\) (or \(t\))
  • Higher confidence level → wider interval
  • Larger sample size → narrower interval
ImportantGrading Focus
  • CI interpretation wording: This is non-negotiable. The correct form is: “We are 95% confident that the true [parameter in context] is between [lower] and [upper].” Deduct for: “There is a 95% chance that \(\mu\) is in this interval” or “95% of the data falls in this interval.”
  • z vs t justification: Cadets must state why they chose z or t. If \(\sigma\) is unknown (almost always), it must be t. Watch for cadets defaulting to z because it’s “easier.”
  • Conditions check: Every CI requires: (1) random sample, (2) independence, (3) sample size condition (CLT). These must appear in the solution.
NoteDeeper Material

What “95% confident” actually means: If we repeated the sampling process many times and built a CI each time, approximately 95% of those intervals would contain the true \(\mu\). The parameter is fixed — it’s the interval that’s random. This is a frequentist interpretation and is notoriously hard for students to internalize.

Why \(n-1\) degrees of freedom: The \(t\)-distribution has heavier tails than the standard normal because \(s\) is a noisy estimate of \(\sigma\). The \(n-1\) degrees of freedom come from estimating \(\mu\) with \(\bar{x}\) — we “use up” one degree of freedom. As \(n \to \infty\), \(t_{n-1} \to Z\).

Sample size determination: To achieve a desired margin of error \(E\): \(n = \left(\frac{z_{\alpha/2} \cdot \sigma}{E}\right)^2\). This requires a prior estimate of \(\sigma\) (from a pilot study or literature). Always round up.

Lesson 19 — Confidence Intervals II

Objectives (SLOs 1, 5, 6):

  • Interpret CIs correctly in context
  • Construct confidence intervals for proportions
  • Assess robustness of CI assumptions
TipKey Concepts
  • Proportion CI: \(\hat{p} \pm z_{\alpha/2} \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{n}}\)
  • Conditions for proportion CI: \(n\hat{p} \geq 10\) and \(n(1-\hat{p}) \geq 10\)
  • \(t\)-Distribution: Heavier tails to account for extra uncertainty when \(s\) estimates \(\sigma\)
ImportantGrading Focus
  • Proportion CI conditions: \(n\hat{p} \geq 10\) and \(n(1-\hat{p}) \geq 10\). Cadets must check these before constructing a proportion CI.
  • Interpretation precision: “We are 95% confident the true proportion of cadets who…” — not “95% of cadets…”
  • Margin of error interpretation: Connect margin of error to the width of the interval and sample size
NoteDeeper Material

Wilson interval: The textbook’s Wald interval (\(\hat{p} \pm z \sqrt{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})/n}\)) has poor coverage for small \(n\) or extreme \(p\). The Wilson interval inverts the test statistic and has better properties. R’s prop.test() uses a continuity-corrected version. Faculty should know why software results may differ slightly from hand calculations.

CI width tradeoffs: Cadets should understand the three knobs: confidence level, sample size, and variability. In practice, only sample size is under the researcher’s control (and it costs money). This is a practical insight worth discussing.

Lesson 20 — Intro to Hypothesis Testing

Objectives (SLOs 5, 6):

  • State \(H_0\) and \(H_a\) for practical questions
  • Compute test statistics and p-values
  • Link significance and \(\alpha\)
TipKey Concepts
  • Framework: \(H_0\) (status quo), \(H_a\) (what we’re trying to find evidence for), test statistic, p-value, decision
  • p-value: Probability of observing a result this extreme or more extreme, assuming \(H_0\) is true
  • Decision rule: Reject \(H_0\) if p-value \(< \alpha\); fail to reject otherwise
  • Type I error (\(\alpha\)): Rejecting \(H_0\) when it’s true
  • Type II error (\(\beta\)): Failing to reject \(H_0\) when it’s false
ImportantGrading Focus
  • Hypothesis notation: \(H_0\) and \(H_a\) must be written in terms of population parameters (\(\mu\), \(p\)), never sample statistics (\(\bar{x}\), \(\hat{p}\))
  • p-value interpretation: “The probability of observing a result this extreme or more extreme, assuming \(H_0\) is true.” NOT “the probability that \(H_0\) is true.”
  • Conclusion language: “Fail to reject \(H_0\)” — never “accept \(H_0\).” This distinction is critical and must be enforced from day one.
  • Context: Conclusions must be stated in the context of the problem, not just “reject” or “fail to reject.”
NoteDeeper Material

Neyman-Pearson framework: The hypothesis testing framework we teach is a hybrid of Fisher (p-values as evidence) and Neyman-Pearson (decision rules with \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\)). Purists from either camp would object to the mashup, but it’s pedagogically standard. The key tension: Fisher saw p-values as a continuous measure of evidence; Neyman-Pearson saw testing as a binary decision procedure.

Power: \(\text{Power} = 1 - \beta = P(\text{Reject } H_0 | H_0 \text{ is false})\). Power depends on the true parameter value, \(n\), \(\alpha\), and \(\sigma\). This is why we can never “prove” \(H_0\) — failing to reject might just mean low power. This concept is tested more in Lesson 25+ but the foundation is here.

The p-value is NOT: (1) the probability \(H_0\) is true, (2) the probability the result is “due to chance,” (3) the probability of making an error. These are common misconceptions across all levels of statistics education — even published researchers get this wrong.

Statistical vs practical significance: A very large sample can produce a statistically significant result (\(p < 0.05\)) for a trivially small effect. Example: a new training program increases PT scores by 0.3 points (\(p = 0.01\), \(n = 10{,}000\)). Statistically significant but practically meaningless. This distinction becomes crucial for WPR II.

Teaching Exercises

NoteSuggested Classroom Demonstrations

CLT Simulation (Lesson 17)

In R (or live on the board), demonstrate the CLT:

  1. Start with a clearly non-Normal population (e.g., Exponential with \(\lambda = 1\))
  2. Take 1,000 samples of size \(n = 5\), compute \(\bar{x}\) for each — plot the distribution
  3. Repeat with \(n = 30\) — show it’s approximately Normal
  4. Connect: “This is why we can do inference about \(\mu\) even when the population isn’t Normal”

Building a CI from Scratch (Lesson 18)

Using class data (e.g., hours of sleep), compute \(\bar{x}\) and \(s\), find \(t^*\) from the table, and construct the interval by hand: \[\bar{x} \pm t^* \cdot \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}\]

Then interpret it correctly as a class. Have a cadet give the interpretation and let the class critique it.

Trial Analogy for Hypothesis Testing (Lesson 20)

Frame hypothesis testing as a trial:

  • \(H_0\): Defendant is innocent (status quo)
  • \(H_a\): Defendant is guilty
  • Evidence = data; beyond reasonable doubt = \(\alpha\)
  • Verdict of “not guilty” ≠ “innocent” (fail to reject ≠ accept \(H_0\))

This analogy resonates with cadets and makes the logic concrete.
