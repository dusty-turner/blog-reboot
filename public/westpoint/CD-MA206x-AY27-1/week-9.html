Lesson Deep Dive

Lesson 32 — Multiple Linear Regression II

Objectives (SLOs 4, 5):

  • Conduct hypothesis tests on individual coefficients
  • Interpret software output for MLR
  • Understand multicollinearity
TipKey Concepts
  • Individual coefficient test: \(t = \frac{b_j}{SE_{b_j}}\) tests \(H_0: \beta_j = 0\) (is this predictor useful after accounting for all others?)
  • Overall F-test: Tests \(H_0\): all slopes are zero (\(\beta_1 = \beta_2 = \cdots = \beta_p = 0\))
  • Multicollinearity: When predictors are correlated with each other — inflates SEs, makes individual tests unreliable
  • Reading R output: summary(lm(...)) gives coefficients, SEs, t-values, p-values, \(R^2\), adjusted \(R^2\), F-statistic
ImportantGrading Focus
  • Software output reading: Cadets must be able to extract the right numbers from R output. Common errors: confusing the overall F-test p-value with individual coefficient p-values, reading the wrong row.
  • Individual vs overall significance: A predictor can be individually non-significant (\(p > 0.05\) for its t-test) but the model can be overall significant (F-test \(p < 0.05\)). This isn’t contradictory — it often indicates multicollinearity.
  • Multicollinearity recognition: If adding a predictor barely changes \(R^2\) but dramatically changes other coefficients, multicollinearity is likely.
NoteDeeper Material

VIF (Variance Inflation Factor): \(\text{VIF}_j = \frac{1}{1 - R^2_j}\) where \(R^2_j\) is from regressing \(x_j\) on all other predictors. VIF > 5 is concerning; VIF > 10 is serious. Remedies: remove one of the correlated predictors, combine them (PCA), or accept wider CIs.

Adjusted \(R^2\) vs AIC/BIC: Adjusted \(R^2\) penalizes for number of predictors but is only useful for comparing models fit to the same data with the same response. AIC and BIC provide more general model comparison metrics. AIC favors prediction accuracy; BIC favors parsimony (stronger penalty for additional predictors).

Interaction terms: \(y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_1 + \beta_2 x_2 + \beta_3 x_1 x_2\) — the effect of \(x_1\) depends on the level of \(x_2\). Interpreting interactions is one of the hardest concepts in regression. Example: the effect of study hours on exam score might depend on whether the cadet attended review sessions.

Lesson 33 — Multiple Linear Regression III

Objectives (SLOs 1, 4, 6):

  • Perform model selection
  • Validate model assumptions with residual plots
  • Communicate regression results
TipKey Concepts
  • Model selection strategies: Forward selection, backward elimination, best subsets
  • Parsimony principle: Prefer simpler models unless a more complex model provides substantial improvement
  • Residual diagnostics: Residuals vs fitted (linearity, constant variance), Q-Q plot (normality), residuals vs each predictor
  • Communicating results: Tables of coefficients, significance levels, \(R^2\); graphs of relationships; plain-language interpretation
ImportantGrading Focus
  • Model comparison justification: Cadets must explain why they chose their final model. “It had the highest \(R^2\)” is insufficient — they should consider adjusted \(R^2\), significance of predictors, parsimony, and residual diagnostics.
  • Residual plots in MLR: Check residuals vs fitted AND residuals vs each predictor. A good fit in one doesn’t guarantee good fit in another.
  • Written communication: Results should be understandable to a general audience (SLO 1). Avoid statistical jargon without explanation.
NoteDeeper Material

Stepwise selection problems: Automated stepwise procedures (forward, backward) are widely used but have known issues: (1) multiple testing inflation — checking many predictors increases false positives; (2) instability — small data changes can produce very different models; (3) overfitting — selected models tend to perform worse on new data. Modern alternatives include LASSO regularization and cross-validation.

Cross-validation: Split data into training and testing sets. Fit the model on training data, evaluate on test data. This gives an honest assessment of predictive performance. K-fold cross-validation repeats this \(k\) times. Cadets won’t implement this, but faculty should understand why “the model that fits training data best” isn’t always the best model.

Transformations: When LINE conditions are violated, transformations can help. Common: \(\log(y)\) for right-skewed responses or non-constant variance; \(\sqrt{y}\) for count data; \(1/y\) for rate data. The Box-Cox transformation finds the optimal power transformation automatically.

Lesson 34 — Peer Review

Objectives (SLOs 1, 6):

  • Critically evaluate peer analyses
  • Provide constructive statistical feedback
TipKey Concepts
  • Peer review focuses on the statistical reasoning, not just the writing
  • Key questions: Is the research question clear? Is the analysis appropriate? Are conditions checked? Are conclusions supported by the analysis?
ImportantGrading Focus
  • Quality of feedback: Cadets should provide specific, actionable feedback — not just “looks good” or “needs work”
  • Statistical critique: Can they identify when a peer’s analysis uses the wrong method, skips conditions, or overstates conclusions?
  • Tone: Constructive and professional — this is practice for real-world collaboration
NoteDeeper Material

Peer review in practice: In academic publishing, peer review is the gatekeeping mechanism for research quality. Reviewers check methodology, statistical validity, and interpretation. Teaching cadets to do this develops SLO 6 (critical consumer) in a practical setting. It also improves their own work — seeing others’ mistakes helps them avoid the same ones.

CautionProject Status Check

By Forge 9, cadets should have:

  • Complete regression analysis (SLR and/or MLR)
  • Residual diagnostics checked
  • Draft of written report in progress
  • Peer review feedback incorporated

Red flags to watch for:

  • Cadets who haven’t started their analysis yet
  • Projects with too few observations for MLR (rule of thumb: at least 10–15 observations per predictor)
  • Reports that present results without checking conditions
  • Conclusions that overstate causation from observational data

Teaching Exercises

NoteSuggested Classroom Demonstrations

Model Selection Live Demo (Lesson 33)

Using a real dataset (mtcars, or a military-themed dataset), walk through model selection on the board:

  1. Start with a full model (all predictors)
  2. Check which are significant; remove the least significant
  3. Refit and compare adjusted \(R^2\)
  4. Check residual plots at each step
  5. Discuss: “Is the simpler model ‘better’? By what criterion?”

Peer Review Calibration (Lesson 34)

Before the peer review, have the whole class review the same sample project together. Discuss what constitutes strong vs weak feedback. This calibrates expectations and ensures consistent quality.

“Good Analysis, Bad Analysis” Comparison (Lesson 33)

Show two analyses of the same data — one that follows best practices (EDA → model → diagnostics → interpretation) and one that skips steps (jump straight to MLR, no residual plots, overconfident conclusions). Have cadets identify the differences and explain why the shortcuts are problematic.
