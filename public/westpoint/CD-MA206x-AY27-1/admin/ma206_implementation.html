Associate Program Director’s Course Execution Guidance

AY27-1 | LTC Dusty Turner, Associate Program Director

1 Purpose

This memo establishes how MA206 will run in AY27-1. It defines the Associate Program Director (APD) and Course Director (CD) roles, describes the two recurring meeting structures – the Forge and the weekly team teaching meeting – and sets expectations for summative assessments.

Given that so much of MA206 is new, the team should start the year with a clear picture of where we are headed and who owns what.

2 Associate Program Director (APD) Responsibilities

The APD owns the intellectual and pedagogical direction of the course. Specifically, the APD will:

  • Set and communicate the vision for what MA206 students should know, understand, and be able to do.
  • Design all summative assessments (see Section 3).
  • Lead the Forge (see Section 5).
  • Establish and oversee the weekly team teaching meeting (see Section 6).
  • Make final decisions on course content, sequencing, and learning objectives.
  • Develop and maintain the mapping between student learning objectives (SLOs) and assessments.
  • Mentor the CD and junior instructors in course leadership.
  • Partner with senior faculty – learn from their experience, seek their input on pedagogy, and create space for them to shape how we teach.

3 Summative Assessments

All summative assessments will be built before the semester starts:

  • The APD will have all versions of WPR 1, WPR 2, and the TEE complete by the end of the summer.
  • The course project will be finalized as well.
  • Each assessment will include a rubric and a document mapping every question back to a student learning objective (SLO).
  • The goal is transparency – instructors will know from day one what expectations we have of students. We will walk a fine line between not teaching to the test and ensuring every instructor understands what to emphasize and what cadets will need to demonstrate on exams.

4 Course Director (CD) Responsibilities

The CD runs day-to-day course operations under guidance from the APD. The APD sets the standard and communicates expectations; the CD translates that guidance into execution. This includes:

  • Building and publishing the WebAssign course, Canvas page, and course calendar before the semester begins.
  • Maintaining WebAssign, Canvas, and the calendar throughout the semester.
  • Tracking course coverage to ensure sections stay aligned on pace and content.
  • Maintaining the course schedule and syllabus.
  • Coordinating make-up exams and enforcing late policies.
  • Serving as the first line of support for instructor questions on content and grading.
  • Providing regular course status updates to the APD – pace, emerging issues, and grade trends.

The CD is the primary point of contact for instructors. When an instructor has a question – whether it is about logistics, content, or how to teach a lesson – the CD is the first stop. The CD will handle operational questions directly and relay content or standards questions to the APD as needed. The APD and CD should maintain a close working relationship – the APD provides direction and the CD keeps the APD informed on how that direction is landing across sections.

5 The Forge

The Forge is the primary teaching lab for the course, led by the APD.

Purpose: Prepare instructors to teach the next block of lessons at the level we expect – not just covering content, but teaching it well.

Structure:

Each Forge has three parts:

  1. Admin (CD-led) – a short block at the opening of each Forge covering course operations. This is the CD’s time to address the team. Typical items include:
    • Coverage and pacing status across sections.
    • Grade distribution updates and any emerging trends.
    • Upcoming deadlines, make-up exam logistics, and schedule changes.
    • Canvas or WebAssign issues that need instructor awareness.
    • Any other operational items that benefit from a face-to-face touchpoint rather than email.
  2. Expectations (APD-led) – a walk-through of the next three lessons covering:
    • What we want cadets to know and be able to do.
    • Relevant WPR and assessment questions tied to those lessons.
    • Specific things we will be looking for when grading.
  3. Depth (APD-led) – a deeper look at a topic beyond what we ask cadets to do. The goal is to strengthen instructors’ own understanding of the material so they can teach it with more clarity and confidence. Instructors who understand the “why” behind the content will be better equipped to explain it, answer questions, and adapt in the classroom.

Leadership:

  • Early on, the APD will personally lead both the Expectations and Depth portions of each Forge for an extended period – to set the standard and demonstrate it week in and week out.
  • When the APD eventually asks others to lead specific portions, the intent and expectations will be clear because they have seen it modeled consistently.

6 Instructor Teams

Instructors will be organized into teams of four. These teams serve two purposes: they are grading teams and instructor development teams. Team composition will be set by the APD based on a mix of seniority, experience, and schedule availability.

Weekly activities:

The specific schedule and expectations will change from week to week, but teams should expect a recurring mix of:

  • Classroom visitations within the team – observing each other teach and providing feedback.
  • Group meetings to share ideas on upcoming lessons and discuss what is working in the classroom.
  • Collaborative grading and rubric refinement to maintain consistency across sections.

Distinction from the Forge:

  • The Forge is top-down: the APD sets the standard and models it.
  • Team time is peer-driven: instructors practice, share, and refine their own teaching within the framework the Forge establishes.
