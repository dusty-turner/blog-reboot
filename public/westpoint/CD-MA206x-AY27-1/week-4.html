Lesson Deep Dive

Lesson 13 — Normal Distribution

Objectives (SLOs 6, 7):

  • Standardize and use normal probabilities
  • Find normal quantiles for given tail areas
  • Assess plausibility using normal models
TipKey Concepts
  • Normal PDF: \(f(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}}\)
  • Standardization: \(Z = \frac{X - \mu}{\sigma}\), then \(Z \sim N(0, 1)\)
  • Empirical Rule: 68-95-99.7% within 1, 2, 3 standard deviations
  • Inverse normal: Given \(P(X \leq x) = p\), find \(x = \mu + z_p \cdot \sigma\)
ImportantGrading Focus
  • Standardization: Every normal probability must show the \(Z = (X - \mu)/\sigma\) step. No credit for “I looked it up” without showing the standardization.
  • Sketch requirement: Cadets should sketch the normal curve, shade the region, and label \(\mu\), \(\sigma\), and the boundary value(s). Sketches prevent sign errors and wrong-tail lookups.
  • Inverse problems: “Find the value such that 90% of observations fall below it” — cadets must use the inverse process. Common error: confusing the \(z\)-score with the \(x\)-value.
NoteDeeper Material

Why the Normal is everywhere: The CLT (Lesson 17) explains it — sums and averages of independent random variables converge to Normal. But even beyond the CLT, the Normal arises as the maximum entropy distribution for a given mean and variance. It’s the “default” distribution when all you know is the center and spread.

Normal approximation to Binomial: When \(np \geq 10\) and \(n(1-p) \geq 10\), \(\text{Binomial}(n, p) \approx N(np, np(1-p))\). The continuity correction (\(\pm 0.5\)) improves the approximation but isn’t required in this course. This is the direct precursor to the proportion z-test in Block II.

QQ plots: Assessing normality graphically using quantile-quantile plots. Points falling on the diagonal suggest normality; S-curves suggest skewness; heavy tails show up as deviations at the extremes. Worth demonstrating even if not heavily tested.

Lesson 14 — Exponential Distribution

Objectives (SLO 7):

  • Recognize the memoryless property
  • Understand the rate-mean relationship
  • Compute exponential probabilities and quantiles
TipKey Concepts
  • PDF: \(f(x) = \lambda e^{-\lambda x}\) for \(x \geq 0\)
  • CDF: \(F(x) = 1 - e^{-\lambda x}\)
  • Mean: \(E(X) = 1/\lambda\); Variance: \(\text{Var}(X) = 1/\lambda^2\)
  • Memoryless property: \(P(X > s + t | X > s) = P(X > t)\)
  • Connection to Poisson: If events follow a Poisson process with rate \(\lambda\), the time between events is Exponential\((\lambda)\)
ImportantGrading Focus
  • Rate vs mean: If the mean time between arrivals is 5 minutes, then \(\lambda = 1/5\). Cadets constantly confuse which is \(\lambda\) and which is \(1/\lambda\).
  • CDF shortcut: Since \(P(X > x) = e^{-\lambda x}\), many problems are faster using the survival function than integrating the PDF. Encourage this.
  • Memoryless property: Cadets must be able to state and apply it. “Given that you’ve already waited 10 minutes, the probability of waiting at least 5 more minutes is the same as the probability of waiting at least 5 minutes from the start.”
NoteDeeper Material

Gamma distribution: The Exponential is a special case of the Gamma distribution (\(\text{Gamma}(1, \lambda)\)). The sum of \(n\) independent Exponential\((\lambda)\) RVs is \(\text{Gamma}(n, \lambda)\). This generalizes the “time until the \(n\)-th event” in a Poisson process.

Hazard function: \(h(x) = f(x) / (1 - F(x))\). For the Exponential, \(h(x) = \lambda\) (constant hazard). This is why the Exponential is memoryless — the “risk” of failure doesn’t change over time. Military application: equipment failure rates and maintenance scheduling.

Reliability engineering: The bathtub curve (decreasing hazard → constant → increasing) describes equipment failure rates. The Exponential models the middle (constant hazard) phase. This connects probability to Army logistics and maintenance planning.

Lesson 15 — WPR I Review

Objectives (SLOs 2, 3, 7):

  • Review and synthesize all Block I material (Lessons 1–14)
  • Practice WPR-style problems under time pressure
ImportantReview Focus

Prioritize these high-error topics:

  1. Distribution identification: Given a scenario, which distribution? (Binomial, Poisson, Normal, Exponential) — this is the single most important skill for WPR I
  2. Stating parameters and conditions: Every distribution application must include conditions check and parameter identification
  3. Expected value and variance calculation: Both discrete (summation) and continuous (integration)
  4. Normal standardization: Draw, standardize, compute
  5. Bayes’ Theorem: At least one problem requiring “reverse the conditioning”

Teaching Exercises

NoteSuggested Classroom Demonstrations

Normal Distribution Z-Score Walkthrough (Lesson 13)

On the board, work through a complete normal probability problem:

  1. Define the variable: \(X\) = cadet PT scores, \(X \sim N(270, 30^2)\)
  2. Find \(P(X > 300)\): standardize \(Z = (300 - 270)/30 = 1.0\)
  3. Sketch, shade, and use the Z-table
  4. Find the score at the 90th percentile: \(x = 270 + 1.28(30) = 308.4\)

Then ask: “How would this change if the SD were 15 instead of 30?” — builds intuition for spread.

Distribution Identification Relay (Lesson 15)

Split the class into teams. Read scenarios one at a time. Teams must write down the distribution (Binomial, Poisson, Normal, Exponential) with justification. First team with correct answer and reasoning gets the point. Great WPR review activity.
