Lesson Deep Dive

Lesson 5 — Exploratory Data Analysis Lab

Objectives (SLOs 1, 2, 4, 6):

  • Execute EDA using appropriate graphs and summaries
  • Justify choices of displays for variable types
  • Communicate findings with clear, concise annotations
TipKey Concepts
  • Match graph type to variable type: histograms/boxplots for quantitative, bar charts for categorical
  • Every graph needs a title, axis labels, and context
  • EDA is about discovery — let the data tell a story before jumping to conclusions
ImportantGrading Focus
  • Graph selection justification: Cadets must explain why they chose a particular display, not just produce it
  • Annotations: Graphs without context (titles, labels, units) are incomplete
  • Written communication: This is the first major SLO 1 assessment — evaluate clarity and completeness of written findings
NoteDeeper Material

EDA philosophy (Tukey): John Tukey coined “Exploratory Data Analysis” in 1977. His key insight: data analysis should begin with looking at the data, not with a model. The investigative process in SLO 1 follows Tukey’s philosophy — form questions, explore data, draw tentative conclusions, refine.

Multivariate EDA: The lab focuses on univariate and bivariate exploration. In real analysis (and in the final project), cadets will need to consider interactions between multiple variables simultaneously. Faceted plots (ggplot2::facet_wrap) and color-coded scatterplots are natural extensions.

Lesson 6 — Probability Basics

Objectives (SLO 7):

  • Compute probabilities of unions, intersections, and complements
  • Understand the Kolmogorov Axioms
  • Apply basic probability rules to solve problems
TipKey Concepts
  • Kolmogorov Axioms: (1) \(P(A) \geq 0\); (2) \(P(S) = 1\); (3) For mutually exclusive events, \(P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B)\)
  • Complement Rule: \(P(A^c) = 1 - P(A)\)
  • Addition Rule: \(P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B)\)
ImportantGrading Focus
  • Set notation: Cadets must use proper notation (\(\cup\), \(\cap\), \(A^c\)) and translate between English and math
  • Mutually exclusive vs independent: These are different concepts — cadets conflate them constantly. Hammer this distinction now.
  • “At least one” problems: \(P(\text{at least one}) = 1 - P(\text{none})\) — watch for cadets trying to enumerate instead of using the complement
NoteDeeper Material

Measure-theoretic probability: The Kolmogorov Axioms are actually about measures on \(\sigma\)-algebras. You don’t need to teach this, but understanding that probability is a measure helps explain why \(P(\emptyset) = 0\), why countable additivity matters, and why we need the axioms at all (not all subsets of \(\mathbb{R}\) are “measurable”).

Frequentist vs Bayesian interpretation: Probability can be interpreted as long-run frequency (frequentist) or degree of belief (Bayesian). The course is primarily frequentist, but the Bayesian interpretation becomes relevant with Bayes’ Theorem in Lesson 7. Worth mentioning to faculty so they’re prepared for cadet questions.

Lesson 7 — Conditional Probability

Objectives (SLO 7):

  • Understand and compute conditional probability \(P(A|B)\)
  • Apply the multiplication rule to find \(P(A \cap B)\)
  • Use the Law of Total Probability
  • Apply Bayes’ Theorem
TipKey Concepts
  • Conditional Probability: \(P(A|B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)}\)
  • Multiplication Rule: \(P(A \cap B) = P(A|B) \cdot P(B)\)
  • Law of Total Probability: \(P(A) = \sum_i P(A|B_i) P(B_i)\) for a partition \(\{B_i\}\)
  • Bayes’ Theorem: \(P(B|A) = \frac{P(A|B) P(B)}{P(A)}\)
ImportantGrading Focus
  • Tree diagrams: Cadets should use tree diagrams to organize conditional probability problems. If they’re not drawing trees, they’re making errors.
  • Bayes’ Theorem direction: The most common mistake is computing \(P(A|B)\) when asked for \(P(B|A)\). Force cadets to explicitly identify what they’re given vs. what they need.
  • Two-way tables: Converting between tables and conditional probabilities should be fluent by end of this lesson
NoteDeeper Material

Base rate neglect: Bayes’ Theorem reveals why rare-event screening tests have high false positive rates even with high sensitivity. Classic example: a drug test with 99% sensitivity and 99% specificity applied to a population with 1% prevalence → P(user | positive) ≈ 50%, not 99%. This is deeply counterintuitive and a great teaching moment.

Prosecutor’s fallacy: Confusing \(P(\text{evidence} | \text{innocent})\) with \(P(\text{innocent} | \text{evidence})\). Real courtroom cases have hinged on this error. Military justice applications make this relevant to cadets.

Lesson 8 — Counting & Independence

Objectives (SLO 7):

  • Count sample spaces of ordered/unordered events with/without replacement
  • Apply the multiplication principle
  • Distinguish between permutations and combinations
  • Understand independent events
TipKey Concepts
  • Multiplication Principle: If task 1 has \(n_1\) outcomes and task 2 has \(n_2\) outcomes, total = \(n_1 \times n_2\)
  • Permutations: Order matters — \(P(n, r) = \frac{n!}{(n-r)!}\)
  • Combinations: Order doesn’t matter — \(\binom{n}{r} = \frac{n!}{r!(n-r)!}\)
  • Independence: \(P(A \cap B) = P(A) \cdot P(B)\) iff \(A\) and \(B\) are independent
ImportantGrading Focus
  • Permutation vs combination: The deciding question is “does order matter?” Cadets must state this explicitly in their work.
  • Independence vs mutually exclusive: Reinforce again — independent means knowing one event occurred doesn’t change the probability of the other. Mutually exclusive means they can’t both happen. If \(P(A) > 0\) and \(P(B) > 0\), mutually exclusive events are never independent.
  • With/without replacement: Affects independence. Drawing cards without replacement → not independent.
NoteDeeper Material

Combinatorial identities: \(\binom{n}{r} = \binom{n}{n-r}\) (choosing what to include = choosing what to exclude). Vandermonde’s identity: \(\binom{m+n}{r} = \sum_{k=0}^{r} \binom{m}{k}\binom{n}{r-k}\). These aren’t tested but help faculty who get combinatorics questions.

Birthday problem: In a room of 23 people, there’s a >50% chance two share a birthday. This is a great demonstration of how probability defies intuition and connects counting to complement rule. If you have a section of ~25 cadets, try it live.

Teaching Exercises

NoteSuggested Classroom Demonstrations

Bayes’ Theorem with M&Ms (Lesson 7)

Bring two bags of M&Ms with different color distributions. Draw one M&M without showing which bag it came from. Ask: “Given this is a red M&M, what’s the probability it came from Bag 1?” Walk through the Bayes calculation live.

Birthday Problem (Lesson 8)

Survey the class for shared birthdays. Then derive the probability using the complement and multiplication principle: \(P(\text{no match among } n) = \frac{365}{365} \cdot \frac{364}{365} \cdot \ldots \cdot \frac{365-n+1}{365}\). Show the dramatic jump from \(n = 22\) (47.6%) to \(n = 23\) (50.7%).

Card Counting Exercise (Lesson 8)

Deal 5-card poker hands and have cadets compute the probability of various hands using combinations. Connects counting to practical probability.
