Lesson Deep Dive

Lesson 21 — One-Sample t-Test

Objectives (SLOs 5, 6):

  • Review the one-sample t-test with a warm-up problem
  • Carry out and interpret hypothesis tests for a population mean
  • Identify the conditions for the t-test
TipKey Concepts
  • Test statistic: \(t = \frac{\bar{x} - \mu_0}{s/\sqrt{n}}\) with \(df = n - 1\)
  • Conditions: (1) Random sample, (2) Independence, (3) \(n \geq 30\) or population approximately normal
  • One-tailed vs two-tailed: Determined by \(H_a\), not by the data
ImportantGrading Focus
  • Complete solution structure: Every hypothesis test must include: (1) Hypotheses in symbols and words, (2) Conditions check, (3) Test statistic computation with work shown, (4) p-value (from R-Lite or table), (5) Decision (compare p-value to \(\alpha\)), (6) Conclusion in context
  • Conclusion template: “At the \(\alpha = 0.05\) significance level, there is [sufficient/insufficient] evidence to conclude that [claim in context].”
  • Connecting CI and HT: If \(\mu_0\) falls outside the 95% CI, we reject \(H_0\) at \(\alpha = 0.05\). Cadets should see this duality.
NoteDeeper Material

Robustness of the t-test: The t-test is fairly robust to non-normality for \(n \geq 30\) (thanks to CLT). For small samples from heavily skewed populations, the t-test can be unreliable — nonparametric alternatives (sign test, Wilcoxon) exist but are beyond course scope. Faculty should know the limitations.

Effect size (Cohen’s d): \(d = (\bar{x} - \mu_0)/s\) measures the practical size of the difference in standard deviation units. Small: \(d \approx 0.2\), Medium: \(d \approx 0.5\), Large: \(d \approx 0.8\). Reporting effect size alongside p-values is increasingly expected in published research.

Lesson 22 — One-Proportion z-Test & Two-Sample Introduction

Objectives (SLOs 1, 5, 6):

  • Review the one-proportion z-test
  • Test \(\mu_1 - \mu_2\) with independent samples
  • Choose pooled vs. Welch procedures
  • Construct and interpret CIs for \(\mu_1 - \mu_2\)
TipKey Concepts
  • One-proportion z-test: \(z = \frac{\hat{p} - p_0}{\sqrt{p_0(1-p_0)/n}}\)
  • Conditions: \(np_0 \geq 10\) and \(n(1-p_0) \geq 10\)
  • Two-sample t-test: \(t = \frac{(\bar{x}_1 - \bar{x}_2) - 0}{\sqrt{s_1^2/n_1 + s_2^2/n_2}}\) (Welch)
  • Pooled vs Welch: Pooled assumes \(\sigma_1 = \sigma_2\); Welch does not. When in doubt, use Welch.
ImportantGrading Focus
  • Proportion test: use \(p_0\) in SE, not \(\hat{p}\): When testing \(H_0: p = p_0\), the standard error uses \(p_0\) (the hypothesized value). For CIs, use \(\hat{p}\). This distinction catches many cadets.
  • Two-sample conditions: Both samples must be random, independent of each other, and each must meet the sample size condition. Cadets often check conditions for only one group.
  • Pooled vs Welch decision: If the problem states “assume equal variances” → pooled. Otherwise → Welch (safer default).
NoteDeeper Material

Why \(p_0\) in the test but \(\hat{p}\) in the CI: Under \(H_0\), we assume \(p = p_0\), so we use \(p_0\) for the SE in the test. For a CI, we have no hypothesized value — we’re estimating \(p\) — so we use \(\hat{p}\). This is a subtle but important distinction that reflects the logic of testing vs estimation.

Welch-Satterthwaite approximation: The Welch \(t\)-test uses a complex degrees-of-freedom formula: \(df = \frac{(s_1^2/n_1 + s_2^2/n_2)^2}{\frac{(s_1^2/n_1)^2}{n_1-1} + \frac{(s_2^2/n_2)^2}{n_2-1}}\). R computes this automatically. Cadets don’t need to memorize this formula — they use software — but faculty should understand why the df isn’t simply \(n_1 + n_2 - 2\).

Behrens-Fisher problem: Testing equality of means when variances are unequal is actually an unsolved problem in exact statistics. The Welch approximation is just that — an approximation. The pooled t-test is exact when \(\sigma_1 = \sigma_2\), which is why the equal-variance assumption matters.

Lesson 23 — Two-Sample t-Test

Objectives (SLOs 5, 6):

  • Review the two-sample t-test for large and small samples
  • Test differences between two means
  • Select appropriate test procedures based on sample sizes
TipKey Concepts
  • Setup: Two independent random samples from two populations
  • Hypotheses: \(H_0: \mu_1 - \mu_2 = 0\) (or \(\mu_1 = \mu_2\))
  • CI for difference: \((\bar{x}_1 - \bar{x}_2) \pm t^* \sqrt{s_1^2/n_1 + s_2^2/n_2}\)
  • Interpretation: “We are 95% confident the true difference in means is between…”
ImportantGrading Focus
  • Independent samples: The two groups must be unrelated. If the same subjects are measured twice → paired (Lesson 24), not two-sample.
  • Direction of subtraction: Define which group is “1” and which is “2” upfront, and be consistent. The sign of the test statistic depends on this.
  • CI interpretation for differences: If the CI for \(\mu_1 - \mu_2\) contains 0, the difference is not statistically significant at that confidence level.
NoteDeeper Material

Practical significance for two-sample tests: Even with \(p < 0.05\), ask “how big is the difference?” A study finding that Method A produces 0.1 more widgets per hour than Method B (\(p = 0.03\), \(n = 5{,}000\) per group) is statistically significant but practically irrelevant. The CI gives a range for the true difference — this is more informative than the p-value alone.

Assumptions hierarchy: (1) Independence between and within groups — most critical, can’t be fixed with large \(n\). (2) Normality — matters for small \(n\), CLT handles large \(n\). (3) Equal variances — only matters for pooled test, Welch handles it. Teach cadets to worry about assumptions in this order.

Lesson 24 — Paired t-Test

Objectives (SLOs 1, 3, 5):

  • Identify paired versus independent designs
  • Test a mean difference using the paired t-test
  • Construct and interpret a CI for a mean difference
TipKey Concepts
  • Paired design: Same subjects measured twice, or natural pairs (twins, before/after, matched)
  • Compute differences: \(d_i = x_{1i} - x_{2i}\), then analyze \(\{d_i\}\) as a single sample
  • Test statistic: \(t = \frac{\bar{d} - 0}{s_d/\sqrt{n}}\) with \(df = n - 1\) (number of pairs minus 1)
  • Key advantage: Pairing removes subject-to-subject variability, increasing power
ImportantGrading Focus
  • Paired vs independent identification: This is a design question, not a computation question. The most important skill is recognizing when data is paired. If in doubt, ask: “Are these measurements linked to the same individual or unit?”
  • Degrees of freedom: \(df = n_{\text{pairs}} - 1\), not \(2n - 2\). Common error.
  • Difference direction: Define the direction of subtraction clearly (e.g., After − Before). The sign of \(\bar{d}\) must match the alternative hypothesis.
NoteDeeper Material

Why pairing increases power: In a two-sample test, the SE includes variability between subjects. In a paired test, each subject serves as their own control, so between-subject variability cancels out. The remaining variability is only the within-subject change. This is why randomized block designs and repeated-measures studies are so common in practice.

When pairing hurts: If the correlation between paired measurements is near zero or negative, pairing can actually reduce power because you lose degrees of freedom (\(n\) pairs instead of \(2n\) observations). This is rare but theoretically possible.

Military application: Before/after training assessments are the canonical paired design. APFT scores before and after a 6-week program, marksmanship before and after a new sight system, etc. These examples resonate with cadets.

WPR II — SLO Discussion

CautionWPR II Coverage — Test Selection Block

This forge introduces four different tests. The critical WPR II skill from these lessons is test selection: given a scenario, cadets must identify the correct test (one-sample t, one-proportion z, two-sample t, or paired t).

Decision flowchart cadets should internalize:

  1. How many samples? (One or two)
  2. What type of data? (Means or proportions)
  3. If two samples: independent or paired?
  4. What are the conditions?

SLO alignment: All objectives hit SLO 5 (inference). SLO 6 (critical evaluation) appears through test selection justification and conditions checking. SLO 1 (communication) is assessed through contextual conclusions.

Recommendation: WPR II should include at least one problem where the test type is not stated — cadets must select it themselves. This is the highest-order skill in Block II.

Teaching Exercises

NoteSuggested Classroom Demonstrations

Test Selection Sorting Activity (Lessons 22–24)

Prepare 10–12 scenario cards. Each describes a study with data. In small groups, cadets sort them into: one-sample t, one-proportion z, two-sample t, or paired t. Then they must justify their choice. Debrief as a class — discuss any scenarios where reasonable people might disagree.

Paired t-Test with Real Data (Lesson 24)

Have cadets measure their resting heart rate, do 20 jumping jacks, then measure again. Compute the differences, run the paired t-test by hand, and interpret. The data is real, the pairing is obvious, and the result is almost always significant.

CI-HT Duality Demo (Lesson 21)

For a one-sample problem, compute both the 95% CI and the hypothesis test at \(\alpha = 0.05\). Show that rejecting \(H_0\) corresponds to \(\mu_0\) falling outside the CI. Then change \(\alpha\) to 0.01 and show the CI widens — now \(\mu_0\) might be inside. Cadets see that \(\alpha\) and confidence level are two sides of the same coin.
