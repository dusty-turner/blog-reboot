Problem 1 — Data Fundamentals (25 points)

SLOs: 1, 2, 3

A researcher at the U.S. Army Research Institute wants to study the effect of sleep deprivation on reaction time. She recruits 40 cadets and randomly assigns 20 to a sleep-restricted group (4 hours of sleep) and 20 to a normal-sleep group (8 hours of sleep). After one night, she measures each cadet’s reaction time (in milliseconds) on a standardized test.

(a) (5 pts) Identify the explanatory variable and the response variable. Classify each as categorical or quantitative.

(b) (5 pts) Is this an observational study or an experiment? Justify your answer.

(c) (5 pts) Can the researcher draw a causal conclusion from this study? Why or why not?

(d) (10 pts) The normal-sleep group has the following five-number summary: Min = 210, Q1 = 235, Median = 258, Q3 = 280, Max = 340.

  • Compute the IQR.
  • Determine whether the maximum value (340) is an outlier using the 1.5 × IQR rule. Show your work.
  • Would the mean or median be more appropriate as a measure of center for this group? Justify your answer using the five-number summary.

Problem 2 — Probability (30 points)

SLOs: 7

At a forward operating base, incoming radio transmissions are classified by priority: Routine (60%), Priority (30%), or Immediate (10%). Due to atmospheric interference, transmissions are sometimes garbled. The probability of a garbled transmission is:

  • 5% for Routine messages
  • 10% for Priority messages
  • 20% for Immediate messages

(a) (5 pts) What is the probability that a randomly selected transmission is garbled? (Use the Law of Total Probability.)

(b) (10 pts) Given that a transmission is garbled, what is the probability that it was an Immediate message? (Use Bayes’ Theorem.)

(c) (5 pts) Are the events “garbled” and “Immediate” independent? Justify your answer mathematically.

(d) (10 pts) A communications sergeant reviews 8 independent Routine transmissions. What is the probability that at most 1 is garbled? Identify the distribution, state the parameters, and show your work.

Problem 3 — Discrete Random Variables (30 points)

SLOs: 3, 7

A squad leader is preparing for a live-fire exercise. Each cadet fires 4 rounds at a target. Based on historical data, each round independently hits the target with probability 0.70.

(a) (5 pts) Let \(X\) = the number of hits out of 4 rounds. What distribution does \(X\) follow? State the parameters and verify the conditions.

(b) (10 pts) Compute \(P(X = 3)\) and \(P(X \geq 3)\). Show your work.

(c) (10 pts) Compute \(E(X)\) and \(\text{SD}(X)\). Interpret \(E(X)\) in the context of this problem.

(d) (5 pts) If the squad has 12 cadets, and each independently fires 4 rounds with the same probability, what is the expected total number of hits for the entire squad?

Problem 4 — Continuous Distributions (45 points)

SLOs: 6, 7

Part A — Continuous Random Variable (20 points)

The time (in hours) a cadet spends studying for WPR I is modeled by the following PDF:

\[f(x) = \begin{cases} cx^2 & 0 \leq x \leq 3 \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}\]

(a) (5 pts) Find the value of \(c\) that makes this a valid PDF.

(b) (5 pts) Find the CDF \(F(x)\).

(c) (5 pts) What is the probability that a cadet studies between 1 and 2 hours?

(d) (5 pts) Find the expected study time \(E(X)\).

Part B — Normal Distribution (15 points)

The Army Physical Fitness Test (APFT) scores for a battalion are normally distributed with mean \(\mu = 265\) and standard deviation \(\sigma = 28\).

(e) (5 pts) What proportion of soldiers score above 300? Show the standardization step and sketch the normal curve with the shaded region.

(f) (5 pts) What score corresponds to the 90th percentile?

(g) (5 pts) A soldier scores 220. Is this score unusual? Justify your answer using z-scores.

Part C — Exponential Distribution (10 points)

Vehicles arrive at a base checkpoint at an average rate of 6 per hour, following a Poisson process.

(h) (5 pts) What is the probability that the time between consecutive arrivals exceeds 15 minutes? Identify the distribution and its parameter.

(i) (5 pts) A vehicle arrived 10 minutes ago and the next one has not yet arrived. What is the probability that you will wait at least 5 more minutes? Name the property you are using.

Problem 5 — Distribution Identification (20 points)

SLOs: 6, 7

For each scenario below, identify the most appropriate probability distribution (Binomial, Poisson, Normal, or Exponential). State the parameter(s) and justify your choice. Do not compute any probabilities.

(a) (5 pts) A supply sergeant inspects 15 randomly selected MREs from a shipment. Each MRE independently has a 0.08 probability of being defective.

(b) (5 pts) Emergency calls to the base fire department arrive at an average rate of 2.5 per day.

(c) (5 pts) The heights of female cadets at West Point.

(d) (5 pts) The time until the next helicopter lands at a helipad, given that helicopters arrive at an average rate of 3 per hour.

Problem 6 — Poisson Distribution (25 points)

SLOs: 3, 7

The number of cybersecurity incidents detected on the West Point network follows a Poisson distribution with a mean of 4 incidents per week.

(a) (5 pts) What is the probability of exactly 2 incidents in a given week? Show the formula and computation.

(b) (5 pts) What is the probability of no incidents in a given week?

(c) (5 pts) What is the probability of 3 or more incidents in a given week? (Hint: use the complement.)

(d) (5 pts) What is the probability of exactly 10 incidents in a two-week period? Be sure to adjust the parameter appropriately.

(e) (5 pts) What are the mean and standard deviation of the number of incidents per week? Interpret the standard deviation in context.
