Lesson Deep Dive

Lesson 36 — ANOVA I

Objectives (SLOs 4, 5, 6):

  • Compare means across multiple groups
  • Conduct and interpret one-way ANOVA
  • Check ANOVA assumptions
TipKey Concepts
  • One-way ANOVA: Tests \(H_0: \mu_1 = \mu_2 = \cdots = \mu_k\) (all group means are equal) vs \(H_a\): at least one mean differs
  • F-statistic: \(F = \frac{MSB}{MSW} = \frac{\text{Between-group variability}}{\text{Within-group variability}}\)
  • ANOVA table: Source | SS | df | MS | F | p-value
  • Assumptions: (1) Independence, (2) Normality within each group, (3) Equal variances across groups
  • Connection to regression: ANOVA is a special case of regression with only categorical predictors
ImportantGrading Focus
  • Hypotheses: \(H_0\) states all means are equal. \(H_a\) states at least one differs — NOT “all are different.”
  • ANOVA only tells you that differences exist: A significant F-test does not tell you which groups differ. Post-hoc tests (Lesson 38) are needed for that.
  • Assumptions check: Equal variance rule of thumb — the largest group SD should be no more than twice the smallest. Also check normality within each group (or rely on CLT if \(n_j \geq 30\)).
  • ANOVA table construction: Cadets should be able to fill in missing entries given partial information (e.g., given SSB, SSW, and group counts, compute df, MS, F).
NoteDeeper Material

ANOVA as regression: One-way ANOVA with \(k\) groups is equivalent to regression with \(k-1\) indicator variables. The F-test in ANOVA is the same as the overall F-test in the regression. This unifies Blocks II and III — everything is a linear model.

Why not just do multiple t-tests? With \(k = 4\) groups, there are \(\binom{4}{2} = 6\) pairwise comparisons. At \(\alpha = 0.05\) each, the family-wise error rate is \(1 - (1-0.05)^6 = 26.5\%\). ANOVA controls this by testing all groups simultaneously at a single \(\alpha\) level.

Random effects vs fixed effects: The ANOVA we teach assumes groups are fixed (specific treatments we chose). If groups are a random sample from a larger population (e.g., randomly selected instructors), the appropriate model is random-effects ANOVA. The F-test construction differs. This is beyond course scope but comes up in experimental design research.

Kruskal-Wallis test: The nonparametric alternative to one-way ANOVA. Doesn’t assume normality — works with ranks instead. Useful when sample sizes are small and normality is questionable. R: kruskal.test().

Lesson 37 — Project IPR (In-Progress Review)

Objectives (SLOs 1, 6):

  • Present in-progress research findings
  • Incorporate feedback into analysis
TipKey Concepts
  • IPR is a practice run — focus on feedback, not evaluation
  • Cadets should present: research question, data description, EDA, analysis (regression/ANOVA), preliminary conclusions
  • Feedback should address: statistical validity, completeness, communication clarity
ImportantGrading Focus
  • Feedback quality matters: If grading the IPR, weight the quality of response to feedback in the final presentation
  • Common IPR issues: Overloaded slides, reading from notes, conclusions unsupported by analysis, missing conditions checks
  • Time management: Set firm time limits (5–7 minutes per cadet) and enforce them

Lesson 38 — ANOVA II

Objectives (SLOs 4, 5, 6):

  • Conduct post-hoc pairwise comparisons
  • Interpret multiple comparison adjustments
TipKey Concepts
  • Post-hoc tests: Only performed after a significant ANOVA F-test
  • Tukey’s HSD (Honest Significant Difference): Compares all pairs while controlling the family-wise error rate
  • Bonferroni correction: Divide \(\alpha\) by the number of comparisons — simpler but more conservative than Tukey
  • Interpretation: “Groups A and B have significantly different means (adjusted \(p = 0.003\)), but groups A and C do not (\(p = 0.42\))”
ImportantGrading Focus
  • No post-hoc without significant F: If the overall ANOVA is not significant, do not proceed with pairwise comparisons — there’s nothing to follow up on.
  • Adjustment method: Cadets must state which adjustment they’re using (Tukey, Bonferroni) and recognize that adjusted p-values are larger than unadjusted ones.
  • Interpretation in context: “The mean [response] for group A is significantly [higher/lower] than for group B by [estimated difference] units.”
NoteDeeper Material

Multiple comparisons problem: The more tests you run, the more likely you are to find a “significant” result by chance. This is the core issue in genomics (testing 20,000 genes), neuroimaging (testing millions of voxels), and any data-rich field. The Bonferroni correction is the simplest fix but is very conservative. False Discovery Rate (FDR) control (Benjamini-Hochberg) is a more modern approach — it controls the expected proportion of false positives rather than the family-wise error rate.

Two-way ANOVA: Extends to two categorical predictors and their interaction. The interaction term tests whether the effect of one factor depends on the level of the other. Example: the effect of training method on PT scores may depend on the cadet’s year group. This connects to interaction terms in MLR.

Contrast coding: Instead of comparing all pairs, researchers sometimes have specific planned comparisons (contrasts). These don’t require the overall F-test to be significant and can be more powerful than Tukey for specific questions. Example: comparing the average of two treatment groups to a control.

Lessons 39–40 — Final Presentations

Objectives (SLOs 1, 6):

  • Present statistical analysis to a general audience
  • Defend methodology and conclusions
TipKey Concepts
  • Presentations assess SLO 1 (communication) and SLO 6 (critical evaluation) more than any other assessment
  • Cadets should demonstrate the full investigative process: question → data → analysis → conclusions
  • Audience questions test depth of understanding
ImportantGrading Focus

Presentation rubric should assess:

  • Research question clarity — Is it specific and answerable with data?
  • Data description — Source, collection method, variables, sample size
  • EDA — Appropriate graphs and summaries
  • Analysis — Correct method (regression, ANOVA, or both), conditions checked, software output interpreted
  • Conclusions — Supported by analysis, limitations acknowledged, scope of inference correct (observational → association, not causation)
  • Communication — Clear slides, appropriate pacing, handles questions well
  • Statistical accuracy — No major errors in methodology or interpretation

Project Discussion

CautionFinal Project Grading Calibration

Before presentations begin, calibrate on these common scenarios:

  • Cadet uses wrong method (e.g., t-test when ANOVA is needed): How much credit?
  • Analysis is correct but conclusions overstate (claims causation from observational data): How much deduction?
  • Beautiful presentation but statistical errors: How to balance?
  • Minimal presentation but solid analysis: How to balance?
  • Missing conditions check: Deduction amount?

Agree on these as a group so grading is consistent across sections.

SLO coverage in project:

SLO How It’s Assessed
1 Presentation quality, written communication, interpretation
2 EDA graphs and summaries
4 Regression/ANOVA model fitting and software output
6 Conclusions supported by analysis, limitations acknowledged

Teaching Exercises

NoteSuggested Classroom Demonstrations

ANOVA with Real Data (Lesson 36)

Compare mean PT scores across four companies (or use a non-sensitive dataset). Run the ANOVA in R, walk through the ANOVA table, interpret the F-test. Then ask: “We know at least one differs — but which ones?” This motivates post-hoc tests in Lesson 38.

Tukey HSD Visualization (Lesson 38)

After running Tukey’s HSD in R (TukeyHSD(aov(...))), show the confidence interval plot. Intervals that don’t contain 0 indicate significant differences. This gives a visual, intuitive summary of all pairwise comparisons.

Practice Presentations (Lesson 37 IPR)

Have 2–3 volunteers present early drafts. The class provides structured feedback using the presentation rubric. This calibrates expectations for everyone and gives the volunteers early feedback.
