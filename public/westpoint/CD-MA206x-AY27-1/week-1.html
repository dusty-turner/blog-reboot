Lesson Deep Dive

Lesson 1 — Intro and Preliminaries

Objectives (SLOs 1, 2, 3):

  • Understand population vs. sample vs. process
  • Learn the relationship between parameters and statistics
  • Distinguish between categorical and quantitative variables
TipKey Concepts
  • Population vs Sample vs Process: Population is the entire group of interest; sample is the subset we observe; process generates data over time (e.g., manufacturing)
  • Parameters vs Statistics: Parameters (\(\mu\), \(\sigma\), \(p\)) describe populations; statistics (\(\bar{x}\), \(s\), \(\hat{p}\)) describe samples. We use statistics to estimate parameters.
  • Variable types: Categorical (nominal, ordinal) vs Quantitative (discrete, continuous)
ImportantGrading Focus
  • Notation: Are cadets distinguishing parameters (\(\mu\), \(\sigma\), \(p\)) from statistics (\(\bar{x}\), \(s\), \(\hat{p}\))? This is the foundation — correct it now.
  • Variable classification: Cadets must correctly classify variables. Watch for confusing “numerical categories” (jersey numbers) with quantitative variables.
  • Sampling methods: Can they identify SRS, stratified, cluster, systematic, and convenience sampling in context?
NoteDeeper Material

Why \(n-1\)? Cadets will encounter Bessel’s correction in Lesson 4, but the intuition starts here. The sample mean \(\bar{x}\) is “closer” to the data than \(\mu\) is, so dividing by \(n\) underestimates the true variance. Using \(n-1\) corrects for this — it makes \(s^2\) an unbiased estimator of \(\sigma^2\). You don’t need to prove this in Lesson 1, but plant the seed: “the denominator will matter when we compute spread.”

Finite Population Correction: For sampling without replacement from a finite population, the variance of \(\bar{X}\) includes a factor \(\frac{N-n}{N-1}\). The textbook ignores this (assumes infinite population or sampling with replacement), but it explains why census data has no sampling error.

Lesson 2 — Sampling & Study Design

Objectives (SLOs 2, 6):

  • Construct and interpret stem-and-leaf displays
  • Create and interpret dotplots
  • Build and analyze histograms and frequency distributions
  • Describe distributions in terms of shape, center, spread, and outliers
TipKey Concepts
  • S-C-S-O: Every distribution description must address Shape, Center, Spread, Outliers
  • Study Design: Observational study vs experiment; random assignment enables causal conclusions
  • Confounding: A lurking variable associated with both the explanatory and response variable
ImportantGrading Focus
  • S-C-S-O: Deduct for incomplete descriptions — all four must be addressed
  • Study design: Can cadets distinguish observational studies from experiments? Do they understand random assignment enables causal conclusions?
  • Graph interpretation: Answers should go beyond “the histogram shows the data” — they must connect the visualization to a story
NoteDeeper Material

Simpson’s Paradox: A trend that appears in several groups of data can reverse when the groups are combined. Classic example: UC Berkeley admissions. This connects to confounding and motivates why study design matters. Consider using this as an enrichment topic if time permits.

Histogram bin width: The choice of bin width fundamentally changes the story a histogram tells. Too narrow → noise; too wide → masks features. There’s no single “correct” choice, but Sturges’ rule (\(k = 1 + 3.322 \log_{10} n\)) gives a reasonable starting point.

Lesson 3 — Measures of Location

Objectives (SLOs 2, 6):

  • Calculate and interpret mean, median, and mode
  • Determine percentiles and quartiles
  • Compare measures of center for different distributions
TipKey Concepts
  • Mean (\(\bar{x}\)): Uses all data, sensitive to outliers
  • Median (\(\tilde{x}\)): Middle value, resistant to outliers
  • Percentiles/Quartiles: Q1, Q2 (median), Q3 divide data into four parts
  • Five-number summary: Min, Q1, Median, Q3, Max
ImportantGrading Focus
  • Mean vs median choice: Cadets must justify which is more appropriate given shape and outliers
  • Contextual interpretation: “The median commute time is 28 minutes” not just “\(\tilde{x} = 28\)”
  • Percentile interpretation: “The 75th percentile means 75% of observations fall at or below this value”
NoteDeeper Material

Weighted means: In many military applications (rating schemes, OERs), means are weighted. The sample mean is actually a special case where all weights equal \(1/n\). If a cadet asks “when would I use a weighted mean?” — performance evaluations and composite scores are natural examples.

Percentile methods: There are multiple conventions for computing percentiles (the textbook uses one; R’s quantile() function has 9 different methods). This can cause small discrepancies between hand calculations and software. Don’t let cadets get stuck on rounding differences.

Lesson 4 — Measures of Variability

Objectives (SLOs 2, 3):

  • Calculate and interpret range and IQR
  • Calculate and interpret variance and standard deviation
TipKey Concepts
  • Range: Max − Min; simple but sensitive to outliers
  • IQR: Q3 − Q1; resistant to outliers, measures middle 50%
  • 1.5 × IQR Rule: Values beyond \(Q_1 - 1.5 \times \text{IQR}\) or \(Q_3 + 1.5 \times \text{IQR}\) are potential outliers
  • Variance (\(s^2\)): Average squared deviation from the mean (divided by \(n-1\))
  • Standard Deviation (\(s\)): Square root of variance; in original units
ImportantGrading Focus
  • SD interpretation in context: “The standard deviation is 3.2 inches” — must include units and meaning
  • Variance/SD calculations: Watch for dividing by \(n\) instead of \(n-1\), and forgetting to square deviations
  • Pairing measures: Report IQR with median (skewed data); report SD with mean (symmetric data). Deduct for mismatched pairings.
  • WebAssign: Check that cadets are showing reasonable completion rates. Flag anyone with zero submissions early.
NoteDeeper Material

Chebyshev’s Inequality: For any distribution, at least \(1 - 1/k^2\) of data lies within \(k\) standard deviations of the mean. So at least 75% within 2 SD, at least 89% within 3 SD. This is weaker than the Empirical Rule (which assumes normality) but works universally. This previews the idea that SD has a universal interpretation.

Why squared deviations? Absolute deviations (\(|x_i - \bar{x}|\)) seem more intuitive. Squared deviations are preferred because: (1) they’re differentiable, which matters for optimization; (2) variance decomposes nicely (variance of sums = sum of variances for independent RVs); (3) the resulting estimator is unbiased.

Teaching Exercises

NoteSuggested Classroom Demonstrations

Population vs. Sample — Class Data Activity (Lesson 1)

Collect a quick variable from the class (e.g., hours of sleep last night). Compute \(\bar{x}\) and \(s\) on the board. Then ask: “Is this a parameter or a statistic? What is the population we care about?”

Use this to reinforce:

  • The class is a sample, not the population
  • \(\bar{x}\) estimates \(\mu\), \(s\) estimates \(\sigma\)
  • Different samples give different values — this motivates the entire course

Board Problem to Walk Through (Lessons 3–4): Given a small dataset (5–7 values), compute the mean, median, variance, and SD step by step. Emphasize the \(n-1\) denominator and ask cadets why we divide by \(n-1\) (degrees of freedom preview).

“Guess the SD” Game (Lesson 4): Show 3–4 dotplots with the same mean but different spreads. Have cadets estimate the SD of each by eye before computing. Builds intuition for what SD “looks like.”
